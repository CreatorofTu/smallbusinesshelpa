## The core thesis — why this exists

Small businesses (especially food & beverage) often reach real product-market fit *faster* than funded startups do — a restaurant knows within days whether people will pay for what it's selling. But nothing preserves the causal record of *what specifically made it work*. When a quiet change breaks it — an ingredient swap, a schedule change, a new competitor, a price change — there's no way to trace the drop back to its cause. The data that would explain it already exists in the business's own sales and reviews; right now it just gets thrown away instead of used.

Most small-business owners — especially the most stubborn, least data-driven ones, exactly the profile least likely to seek this out — have never had AI show up as anything but an image generator. This product is meant to be the first thing that shows up as an actual business partner instead.

**Mission line, keep verbatim wherever the pitch needs one sentence:** *"Nobody's time in the sun should end in a year — it lasts forever."*

**Sharper articulation of the goal, added later — the owner's own time back, not just survival:** the point of the AI isn't only "help the business not die," it's to let the owner actually walk away from the day-to-day and get real hours of his own day back. The founder's own words: *"the goal of the ai is to allow the boss to walk away for more time out the day."* This isn't immediate — see "Baseline timing" below for the phased trust model this implies (hands-on during the baseline period, then actively pushed toward stepping back once the system has earned trust).

## The core mechanic

1. Track the business against the exact **version** of itself at any given moment — recipe/ingredients, price, hours. Literally version control for a physical, real-world product.
2. When a number moves — customers and sales are tracked **separately, never blended**, since "fewer customers" and "same customers spending less" are different facts needing different follow-up questions — ask one plain question tying the change to the most recent logged version diff: *"did you touch the recipe?"*
3. Package the answer into one concrete, dollar-and-customer directive: *"removing the brown sugar saved you $15 but cost you 4 customers this week — about $60 in the register."*
4. Tone is load-bearing: impartial, never a judge. Good or bad, the owner should know why. No red/alarm styling on a "bad" result, no punitive framing anywhere.

## Strategic pivot: F&B only, not broad multi-industry

Mid-session, deliberately narrowed scope from "all small businesses" to **food & beverage only — restaurants and online food sales.**

Reasoning (the founder's own articulation, worth preserving because it's the actual answer to "why F&B, though" when that question comes up):
- F&B carries two separate, usually-conflated reputations: thin margins/high failure rates (the real reason investors avoid it) and "nobody there wants help" (false — the founder's own father disproves this daily).
- Because food service has the lowest technical barrier to entry, it's the default first business for millions of people — which is exactly why it's the *least* served by good causal-insight tooling, not despite being oversaturated but almost because of it.
- Named risk this doesn't erase on its own: restaurant SaaS has a real churn problem (a customer going out of business isn't the product's fault) and thin willingness-to-pay. The product's actual answer to that isn't cosmetic — "helps you not go out of business" makes the product's success metric and the customer's survival the same metric, which is a structurally different bet than most restaurant software makes.

## Scope decision: onboarding branches by business type

This section consolidates three related moments from this project's history: the original two-path onboarding recommendation, a later scope-narrowing question, and its resolution.

### Original recommendation: a thin two-path fork (restaurant vs. online food sales)

The founder asked directly whether onboarding should branch into two paths — "I have a restaurant" vs. "I sell a product online" — given the F&B-only pivot already names exactly these two sub-types.

**Recommendation given in that session:** yes, branch, but as a thin fork on one shared core, not two separate products.
- Shared and identical for both: daily customers/sales numbers, the comparison engine, directive generation, push notifications, the account/business-profile shell.
- The one branch point: a single onboarding question — "Which best describes your business? Restaurant / Online food sales" (reuses the existing tap-toggle widget).
- What that one answer changes: (1) the daily-log "what changed" placeholder/example text — already fully designed (restaurant: recipe/ingredients/hours; online: packaging/shipping/platform); (2) at most one additional type-specific onboarding question each (restaurant: walk-in/reservation/both; online: which platform — Shopify/Etsy/DoorDash/Instagram/own site); (3) eventually, minor wording differences in the generated directive sentence ("customers" vs. "orders").
- Explicitly recommended against building two fully separate onboarding flows or data models — the difference between the two business types is genuinely small (one profile field, a couple of copy variants), and duplicating the comparison/directive logic for it would be pure maintenance cost with no product benefit.

### Later question: narrow further, to restaurant/cafe/caterer? — **decided: restaurant/cafe only, caterer cut**

The founder raised a further scope question in his own words: rather than an open-ended rolepicker, narrow onboarding specifically to named business types — reasoning that "we may not need to offer so much options of front we need to stay true to thesis... lets just be the michlin star." Caterer was floated as a third type, then explicitly cut once the mechanic-fit conflict below was surfaced — the founder's own call, verbatim: *"scrap it."*

**The case for narrowing (restaurant/cafe):**
- A closed, known set lets onboarding copy, examples, and defaults get concrete instead of generic: each type gets literal QR-placement guidance (one code per table for a restaurant; fewer per-seat codes and more weight on the counter/register code for a cafe, since seats usually aren't "owned" the way a restaurant table is), pre-populated environment-variable defaults, and its own onboarding branch questions — none of which is possible when the range of business shapes is unknown.
- Restaurant and cafe share the same underlying topology — a fixed, owned physical space the owner can walk, narrate, and place stickers in — but aren't the same business: a cafe is typically counter-first, shorter-dwell, more walk-in, often with communal/unassigned seating rather than an owned table. Its QR placement and environment-variable weighting genuinely differ from a restaurant's, not just its copy.
- The real breakpoint this surfaces isn't "exactly N types" — it's whether a business type has a walkable, owned physical space to instrument at all. Restaurant and cafe both clear that bar; that's exactly why caterer didn't.

**Why caterer was cut:** the founder's own definition of a caterer in the same message — "selling of a good with out a set space-environment" — directly contradicted the flagship mechanic the QR/environment-review feature depends on: an owner walking one stable, owned space, voice-narrating what's there, then placing stickers that get scanned repeatedly, over time, at the same fixed real-world spot. A caterer working a different venue every booking has no fixed spot to return a code to, no long-term ownership of the space to sticker, and no single persisting "environment" the way a restaurant's dining room or a cafe's counter does. Once this was surfaced, the founder cut it directly rather than force an adapted, heavier build (codes traveling with the setup, re-declared per event) — restaurant/cafe only, decided.

### Still open: does "restaurant/cafe" replace or coexist with "online food sales"?

Restaurant/cafe doesn't mention online food sales at all: online food sales means e-commerce/shipped goods with no physical environment (Shopify, Etsy, DoorDash, a shipped subscription box) — a different axis entirely from "does this business have a walkable space." Whether online food sales survives as a third rolepicker option alongside restaurant/cafe, or is intentionally cut for v1, is genuinely unresolved and needs the founder's own call, not a silent merge.

A strict restaurant/cafe list also excludes real physical-space F&B businesses that don't cleanly fit either label: food trucks and mobile carts, grab-and-go bakeries/delis with no seating, ghost/delivery-only kitchens (no customer-facing environment at all), food-hall or shared-kitchen stall vendors (don't own their environment), farmers-market/pop-up vendors, and drink-forward bars/breweries with a food program. Not a blocker, just worth naming as the real cost of staying at exactly two types for v1.

Everything else in the confidence-gate pipeline (QR/environment-item signal, wait-time, the core-product sub-block, and now the diff log itself) has real inputs.

## Architecture: GitHub for version control, Vercel KV for operational data

Real, load-bearing decision, not decoration — it makes the tagline literally true instead of a metaphor. Two independent sessions (this build session and a separate design-conversation session) converged on the identical split without coordinating, which is a good sign it's the right call:

- **Lives in GitHub, one private repo per business, auto-created at the end of onboarding:** the version-tracked business profile only. Every onboarding save or edit = one real commit, message like "Updated brown sugar in Belgian Waffles." This is exactly the class of data that's supposed to be versioned — infrequent, meaningful, benefits from real diff/history.
- **Stays in Vercel KV, not GitHub:** daily sales/customer counts, push subscriptions. High-frequency and operational, not "versions" of the business — committing this daily would spam the repo and burn GitHub API rate limits (GitHub's API is not built for high-frequency transactional writes) for zero pitch value. Version control should mean something when the log is actually read, not be noise. **Confirmed low-risk at real scale:** the repo is passive storage/context for an AI to reference, not a live per-interaction workflow engine — writes only happen on meaningful changes. At 1,000 businesses making a handful of commits a day each, that's comfortably under GitHub's 5,000 requests/hour personal-token limit (verified 2026) even before migrating to a GitHub App for higher headroom; the only real risk is a bursty launch day (many signups firing setup commits in the same hour), not steady-state load.
- **Repo-per-business also doubles as the future multi-tenancy boundary** (see the existing "no accounts yet" blocker above), and makes the repo itself a literal, open-and-show artifact for a pitch or interview.
- **Security, non-negotiable:** repos created private; the GitHub token that creates/writes them lives server-side only — `.env.local`, gitignored, Vercel env var, never shipped to the client. Same treatment as the existing VAPID keys. One human action required: generate a fine-grained GitHub Personal Access Token scoped to repo creation + contents read/write, on the founder's own account.

### Repo layout, refined — environment as the shell, not a peer folder

The founder's own words, worth preserving for the reasoning: *"the item variable list found in the environment, tables, radios, etcetera. The core variable list that is the core product being sold and its recipe. And the things that go with it, and the environment, which is the shell, labeled the business name."* This refines the layout above: "environment" isn't a folder alongside `core-products/`, it's the container both live inside — the repo root itself, named for the business, *is* the environment.

- **`business.json` (repo root):** hours, price, address — properties of the business existing at all, not a product and not a physical item.
- **`core-products/<name>.json` (unchanged):** one file per recipe/product. "The things that go with it" (sides, toppings, modifiers) nest as a field inside the product's own file rather than a separate folder — a side has no meaning independent of the product it rides on.
- **`environment-items/<item-slug>.json` (new):** the physical, QR-stickered objects — one file per table, radio, or anything else in the sticker walkthrough. This is also naturally where each QR code's variable-binding lives (label, location, QR id, notes).

**Grounding, not just relabeling:** this maps onto real, established theory, not an invented framework. The recipe/core-product vs. environment split is Kotler's core-vs-augmented-product model; the physical-environment-as-outcome-lever piece is Mary Jo Bitner's "servicescape" theory (the physical environment of a service business genuinely shapes customer perception and behavior — not decoration). The founder's three-way split is a real, sharper factoring of both, not a simplification.

### The confound problem, and its fix — a confidence-gate design for the directive engine (designed, not yet built)

The core mechanic (see "The core mechanic" above) is a legitimate before/after causal design — log a version diff, watch the outcome move, attribute the move to the diff — and git is a genuinely good substrate for it (a real timestamped record of one class of intervention, for free). But as designed originally it had one classic vulnerability any experimentalist would flag: **confounded variables.** Attribution is only trustworthy when exactly one thing changed between two measurement points. The moment an owner changes the recipe *and* the hours in the same week — not a rare edge case for a small operator — "the most recent logged diff" becomes ambiguous between two live causes. Two smaller, related gaps sit next to it: the commit timestamp is whenever the owner got around to logging the change, not when the kitchen actually started serving it differently — a fuzzy event time compared against day-granularity outcome data even in the clean single-variable case; and there's no accounting for lag — a recipe change's effect (word of mouth, a repeat-visit swing) might show up two weeks later, not in the very next logged diff, and "most recent" quietly assumes effects are instantaneous, which they usually aren't.

**Fix, designed with the founder's explicit go-ahead — a confidence gate, not a redesign.** This is a real, separate axis from the severity tiers already documented (severity = how big/urgent the finding is; confidence = how sure the engine is *why* it happened) — a directive can be high-severity and low-confidence at the same time, and the copy needs to say so honestly rather than collapse the two into one number.

1. **Variable-isolation check.** Look at every tracked-variable diff (across `core-products/`, `environment-items/`, `business.json`) since the last outcome measurement, not just the single most recent one. Exactly one changed → proceed to a confident attribution. More than one changed → the directive names *all* of them and says so plainly ("you changed both the recipe and your hours this week — could be either, or both") rather than picking one and stating it as fact.
2. **Lag window, not just "most recent."** Check diffs across a wider trailing window (e.g. the last 14 days, not only since the last measurement), and prefer whichever change's timing best lines up with when the outcome actually started moving — an older change with a tighter timing match beats a newer one with a looser one. This directly answers the "effects aren't instantaneous" gap above.
3. **Noise floor from the business's own baseline.** Use the 7-day baseline (see "Baseline timing" above) to establish this specific business's normal day-to-day swing size, and require an outcome move to clear that floor before it's treated as a real signal at all — a low-volume business's baseline naturally has a lower bar to clear than a high-volume one, so this scales per-business rather than using one flat threshold for everyone.
4. **Three confidence tiers, driving the copy directly:**
   - **High** — exactly one variable changed, its timing matches the outcome move, and the move clears the noise floor → a direct causal directive, stated as fact ("removing the brown sugar cost you 4 customers this week").
   - **Medium** — either multiple candidate variables changed in the window, or the timing match is loose → a hedged directive naming the real candidates rather than asserting one ("something in the last two weeks — the recipe change or the new hours — is the likely cause; here's what changed").
   - **Low / none** — the move doesn't clear the noise floor, or nothing was logged in the relevant window → no directive at all, just "keep logging" (matching the existing honest pre-baseline behavior, extended to this case too).

Not yet built — no directive-generation code exists at all today (the real `/api/log-summary` is still pure arithmetic, per "What's real and deployed right now" above). This is the concrete spec for when it is.

### Hard rule, corrected in after the fact: staffing/process variables must be anonymized before they ever reach the reasoning engine

An idea floated in this same conversation — treat staffing/process issues as a candidate variable in the confound-gate, sourced from the owner's own free-text daily note ("Sarah called in sick," "short-staffed tonight") — was proposed without a safeguard it actually needs, and is corrected here rather than left standing as originally stated. The founder's own catch: *"is this not illegal to attach to the employees tho... would it border abuse."*

This is the exact same risk shape already governed elsewhere in this doc — the "hard chat" workforce idea was deliberately kept at team level, not individual, specifically to avoid becoming a surveillance product, and the employee-directive idea flags that a named-employee log is "an individual-level monitoring log in substance... arriving through a side door" even when it isn't framed as scoring. A staffing variable that quietly builds a causal record pointing at a named person, with no consent, no disclosure, and no way for that person to know or respond, is that same side door. The legal exposure here is real but genuinely jurisdiction-dependent (several states have real, growing employee-monitoring disclosure requirements) — not verified to a specific statute, and not the point regardless: this borders abuse independent of whether a specific law catches it.

**Hard rule, matching the same discipline already used for reviewer comments elsewhere in this doc: generalize, never name.** If an owner's note contains an employee's name, the reasoning engine must only ever see and reason about the generalized operational state ("the business was short-staffed") — the name must be stripped or redacted *before* the text reaches the model, not merely omitted from what gets shown back to the owner. This needs to be enforced in code (a redaction pass on the note text before it's ever assembled into the prompt context), not left as a prompt instruction the model might not reliably follow.

### Standing rule: back-of-house/operational insight comes through customer engagement, never through employee surveillance

Extending the same principle further, after a second surveillance-adjacent idea was floated and corrected in this same conversation (an "instruct employees to move faster and time their output" framing for wait-time data — rejected for the same reason as the named-staffing variable, even without naming anyone: monitoring aggregate worker speed and pressuring people to go faster is the surveillance pattern itself, not just a name attached to it). The founder's own resolution, worth locking in as a standing rule rather than re-litigating each time this tension resurfaces: *"lets make it a standing rule that we can break outside of the core product focus not through surveillance but through customer engagement so using the customers voice as a metric but honing it and directing through relevantly curated questions that ellict the intended goal of gather data to tell them about their back of house without really being back there."*

**Concretely: any signal about what's happening behind the scenes — staffing, pacing, timing, process — must be sourced from the customer's own voice via curated questions, never from directly monitoring, timing, or instructing employees.** The QR/review system already being built is the one legitimate channel for this — the customer-tapped wait-time idea above is the first concrete example (ask the *customer* how long they waited, don't time the *staff*). ### Baseline timing — three unreconciled proposals, none matching what's actually implemented

- **Proposal 1 (original): 30–60 day baseline** before the system trusts its own directives enough to act autonomously at all — deliberately framed as an honest feature ("most AI-insight products ship day-one confidence on zero-history data").
- **Proposal 2: 14-day baseline + owner-set goal** — show the owner their real current average, ask them what they want it to look like, and have the directive engine work toward *their* stated target rather than a number the AI decided was "good." Open question: what happens when an owner sets an unrealistic goal — the system needs to say so honestly (same impartial-partner instinct as everywhere else), not silently agree to chase something that was never realistic. Also open: 14 days may be plenty of signal for a high-volume business and not enough for a low-volume one — confidence probably needs to keep sharpening past day 14 for slower businesses rather than being hard-locked at exactly two weeks.
- **Proposal 3: same-day/first-7-day-window lock** — the founder's own reasoning, worth preserving verbatim: *"why cant we get baseline data same day to 7 day schedule, its more work, but its time... Your starting base is your base line, we cant reverse shit... our bet is we can keep them from a bad ending if we start tracking now as mechanism for preservation of their small business. nothing inflated about it. its the core."* The core claim: the product's honest job is prevention, not reversal — it can never undo a decline that already happened, only catch the next one early — so the earlier the baseline locks, the more literally true the "before they leave forever" pitch becomes. Proposed mechanism: lock the baseline from the first real week of logging alone (one 7-day window, not two), then every subsequent day compares the trailing 7 days against that fixed baseline — roughly a week faster to first real insight than today's actual implementation.
- **What's actually implemented today:** two full separate 7-day windows (4+ logged days in each) before any directive — matching none of the three proposals above cleanly. Reconciling all four (three proposals + the real code) into one model is still open.
- **Why the 7-day window specifically, in the founder's own words — the phased-trust model this baseline period is actually for:** *"he should certainly be there during the 7 day data baseline building times to... keep the bar high, but after that we should push him to walk away and let us manage and he can communicate with employees what we tell him and ofc let us buy things if needed."* Concretely: the owner stays hands-on and engaged during the baseline week (this is deliberate, not a gap to shrink), and once the baseline is established, the product should actively encourage the owner to step back — the AI runs day-to-day, the owner relays what the AI tells him to employees (see the manager-relay resolution below), and the AI can spend money when needed (the confirm-then-delay + Stripe/Ramp agent-card mechanic above). This is the real, human reason behind the phased-trust structure already designed — not just "an honest feature," but the actual product goal from "The core thesis" above: give the owner his time back, not just keep the business alive.
- **Proposal 4 — DECIDED: a two-tier provisional/full baseline, reconciling faster time-to-value with real statistical validity.** Founder-confirmed ("done. consider it done."), superseding Proposals 1-3 above as the chosen path — this is the model to build, not one candidate among four anymore. A restaurant's traffic isn't flat across the week (Tuesday vs. Saturday are structurally different business), which is exactly why 7 days was chosen originally — it's the minimum window guaranteed to cover every day-of-week once. Straight-up compressing to 2-3 days breaks this: a baseline built from, say, Mon/Tue/Wed has no idea what a normal Saturday looks like, and would misfire the first time one occurs. The fix, matching the confidence-gate tiering above rather than inventing a new mechanic: a **provisional baseline** (2-3 days) gives the owner a rough overall-average signal almost immediately, explicitly flagged low-confidence since it hasn't seen a full week yet; a **full baseline** (once every day-of-week has actually been logged at least once) is the real, trustworthy one that powers confident directives. This directly answers the "why does the owner wait 7+ days for anything" concern raised elsewhere in this doc, without pretending a 3-day sample knows what Saturday looks like. See "Two time-to-value channels" further below for how this pairs with early QR-derived positive feedback for an even faster first win.

## New feature idea: QR-code environment-review system (raw idea, not yet built)

The founder's own idea, verbatim, preserved per this doc's own convention for raw brain-dumps: *"we develop two types of qr codes, one specially for variables that make up the environment (so customers can see a qr code and comment on that specific variable directly if they want eg. a radio would have a qr code that says Radio: Hows our music, is this placed properly?) 2. the other qr code is simply for the doors and for the owner to place where convenient for them at doors at the regist etc, We need a separate prefixed version of the just add egg HTML to hold said specific request... because I plan on sending QR code stickers to the restaurants via mail or in person handoff... **Pricing gate, decided:** this entire feature — both QR code types, the variable-binding system, the sticker walkthrough — is **100% a $50/month-tier feature**, per the founder's own words (see "Billing model" above). The $20 entry tier gets a lighter, aggregate review-synthesis signal only, not this system.

### Two QR-code types

- **Variable-targeted code** — physically placed at one specific tracked environment variable's real-world location (the founder's own example: a radio, for ambiance). The code's destination URL would already encode which variable it's about, so the landing page opens pre-scoped to a specific question ("how's the music today?") instead of a blank box, with a fast tap-a-reaction control (better/same/worse, or a short scale) and an optional free-text field below.
- **General-purpose code** — placed at a universal touchpoint (the door, the register), not tied to any one variable, for the owner to place wherever convenient. Because there's no single thing to anchor to, the landing page has to ask an open question ("how was your visit?") paired with a fast first-tap sentiment capture (thumbs up/down or stars), with free text optional and secondary.
- The difference between the two is pre-scoping, not page mechanics — a targeted code's page already carries context the customer didn't have to supply; a general code's page has to stay broad and lean more on free text actually getting used, since one generic sentiment tap alone doesn't say what the visit was about.
- Baseline UX bar for both, consistent with the app's existing posture: no login wall ever for the customer (a different, anonymous, one-time actor from the owner — never conflated with any owner-side account work), one tap to a usable signal with free text always optional, mobile-first single-column with large tap targets, one plain sentence up front on who sees the comment and what happens to it, and a visible confirmation on submit since there's no account to check back through otherwise.

### Every scan carries a second, universal block about the core product — including an informal payment self-report

**New refinement, decided in shape though the founder flags its own accuracy limit honestly.** Regardless of which variable a code is scanned for, the landing page carries two layers, not one: item-specific questions (whatever the scanned variable is about) *plus* a second, always-present sub-block about the core product itself. The founder's own example set, worth preserving as illustrative real copy:

- **Item-specific layer, e.g. a speaker's code:** *"How's the music? / How was it last time you were here? / What would you prefer?"*
- **Universal core-product layer, e.g. for a waffle business:** *"Did you have a waffle? / How much did you pay for that waffle? / What all did you get on that waffle? / Was the waffle the same as you had it last time? / Does it taste different?"*

**The realization behind this, in the founder's own words:** *"People who scan the QR codes all will have a sub input box, regardless of whatever variable they scan, and that sub input box is about the core product. All we got to ask is: did you pay for a core product? How much did you pay?, thats how we track money — not so accurate if people dont do it but we can realllly try and make the qr codes attractive and to die for."* This is a genuinely new mechanism: an informal, crowd-sourced way to gather rough price/revenue signal directly from customers, as a supplement to the owner's own self-reported daily sales — not a replacement for it, and explicitly **not** real payment processing (this doc already states the real app has zero payment capability and defers real POS integration — this is a self-reported customer survey question, not a transaction, and must never be described or built as anything more accurate than that). The founder's own honesty about its limits ("not so accurate if people don't do it") should carry through into any copy or documentation describing this feature — never oversell it as reliable financial data.

Also worth its own line, since it directly bears on whether this self-report data is even collected often enough to be useful: the "was the waffle the same as last time? does it taste different?" questions are themselves a live, customer-side check on the causal mechanic's own core claim — real-time qualitative corroboration (or contradiction) of whatever the owner's own logged version diff says changed.

**Real caveat on the core-product question, worth recording explicitly:** a "no" answer to "did you have a [core product]?" is genuinely just as important as a "yes" — arguably more informative. The founder's own reasoning: *"getting a no is genuinely just as important as yes because why DONT you have a waffle in a waffle restaurant lol."* In a business whose core product is exactly what it's known for, a customer visiting and NOT ordering it is itself a real, flaggable signal (price sensitivity, menu confusion, a bad recommendation, something else entirely) — not a null result to discard. Any synthesis logic built on top of this question must treat "no" as a distinct, meaningful signal worth surfacing to the owner, not just filter it out as non-response.

### Technical shape

- A QR code only encodes a URL — "a separate prefixed version of the app's HTML," in the founder's own words, most naturally maps to one stable route per (business, variable) pair, something like `/r/<businessId>/<variableSlug>`, with the general code being the same route with no variable slug — one lightweight page parameterized client-side by whatever's in the URL, not a separate generated artifact per code.
- That page would need to be a genuinely new, separate static file (sibling to the app's `index.html`, not a route inside it) — it must not register the service worker or link the app manifest (a customer scanning a wall-mounted code should never get an "Add to Home Screen" prompt), and it shouldn't inherit any of the owner-facing app's state or chrome.
- Submissions would need a new public, unauthenticated, per-business-scoped API endpoint, following the same shape as the app's existing `/api/log-entry` and `/api/save-profile` (validate input, cap body/text size, server-assigned timestamp, generic error responses). Today's endpoints write to flat global keys with no business ID anywhere (there's genuinely only one business in the system right now) — this feature is inherently multi-business by design, so it can't reuse that pattern as-is.
- Storage would sit in Vercel KV, not the GitHub version-control layer — comments are high-frequency, per-visit, operational data, the same category the daily log's own customer/sales counts are already kept out of the repo for. Only the variable's own identity (name, which product/area it belongs to) would be a real version-controlled fact.
- Turning raw comments into a synthesized takeaway would require a new LLM call — nothing in this app's `api/` today calls out to a model at all (the daily-log comparison is pure arithmetic). This would be genuinely new infrastructure, not an extension of something live.
- **The physical variables named above (radio, and by extension things like seating/menu) are not the app's currently tracked environment variables** — those are recipe/ingredients, price, and hours. Whether ambiance-type variables get formally added to the tracked set, and where owners would even create/manage them (there's no settings screen anywhere in the app today), is unresolved.

### QR visual style — decided: scan reliability wins over branding, every time

A live test rendered three styled QR codes (black-on-cream with rounded dots + center logo, black-on-white same style, and a plain classic square as a control) and decoded all three programmatically to check they actually scan, not just look right. **The plain square decoded correctly; both styled versions (rounded dots + center logo) failed to decode** with the verification tool used. Inconclusive on the exact cause (the decoder used is a known-weaker library than a real phone camera, so this could be a tooling artifact rather than a real-world failure) — but the founder's own call, given that uncertainty: *"go safe always."*

**Standing rule for this feature, not just a one-off pick:** scan reliability outranks brand styling, unconditionally. Default to the plain, classic square QR style (proven to decode) unless a specific styled variant has been physically verified scanning correctly on a real phone camera, in real print, not just on-screen. Any future styling (color, rounded dots, center logo) needs that real-device verification pass before it's trusted for real printed stickers — being rendered and looking correct on screen is not sufficient proof, per the test above.

### Resolved: one generic scan page for every variable, questions generated once at binding time — not per scan

Owner-entered item labels during the walkthrough are free text ("3 tables, 12 chairs, 2 speakers") — there is no fixed list to hand-write per-variable question sets against, and with potentially dozens of labeled items per business, a separate hardcoded page per variable was never viable ("we dont need to make 45 html pages for 45 variables"). Resolved shape: **one single generic scan page** (per the `/r/<businessId>/<variableSlug>` route above) reads two things at render time — (1) this variable's question set, generated **once, by an LLM call made at binding time** (the moment the owner assigns a sticker to a labeled item during onboarding, not on every future customer scan — no per-scan AI cost, no drift in what a repeat customer sees), stored alongside that variable's record; and (2) the fixed, non-AI-generated universal core-product block (see "Every scan carries a second, universal block about the core product" above), which is the same static template every time and never needs generation.

**The actual prompt, to run once per labeled variable at binding time:**

```
You are writing the short set of questions a customer sees after scanning a QR code
attached to one specific labeled item inside a small restaurant or cafe.

Inputs:
- Item label, exactly as the owner typed it, unedited: {{itemLabel}}
- Core product: {{coreProduct}}
- Business type: {{businessType}} (restaurant or cafe)

Write 2-3 short, plain-language questions specifically about this item — its condition,
its placement, whether it adds to or takes away from the experience. Match this exact
tone and length (real examples already in use):

- Radio/speaker: "How's the music? How was it last time you were here? What would you prefer?"
- Table: "How's this table? Tell us something about it? Do you love where you're sitting?
  What would make you love sitting here? Do you hate sitting here?"

Rules:
- Max 3 questions — 2 is often enough for a simple item.
- Plain, warm, impartial. Never leading, never implying something is wrong.
- Never ask about price or the core product — that's a separate, fixed block appended
  after yours, not your job to write.
- If the label is vague or you genuinely can't picture the physical object (e.g. "misc",
  "thing 1"), don't guess — fall back to one single generic question:
  "How's the {{itemLabel}}?"

Return only the questions, one per line. No numbering, no commentary.
```

Not yet built — this is the concrete spec for wherever the binding-time step ends up living (likely the same sticker-assign screen already in onboarding.html).

### Trust & safety

- **Highest-value risk: sabotage of the causal record itself, not just spam.** The product's whole mechanic is tying a sentiment signal to a recent logged version change ("did you touch the recipe?"). A timed burst of fake negative comments right after a real price/recipe change could hand the owner a false directive that looks like fact — a materially worse failure mode here than ordinary review-bombing, because the product's value proposition is "trust this causal read." Any synthesized claim would need cross-checking against the business's own structured daily numbers before being packaged as a directive, not accepted on comment volume alone.
- No endpoint in this app has any rate limiting today. A public QR-fed form is a new class of exposure (anyone with the URL — scanned or not, since a QR code carries no real secrecy — can submit), and any limiting would need to be IP- or session-scoped, not account-scoped, since there's no real auth to key off of. A shared-wifi dining room means IP-only throttling would false-positive on a legitimate full house, so a per-code-instance cap alongside a per-IP cap is worth considering.
- Same input-validation bar the app's other public writes already hold themselves to: capped body/text size, server-assigned timestamps, escaped output, and — if comments ever feed an AI prompt — the free text must go in fenced, never interpolated raw.
- No autonomous action off unmoderated public input: a burst of comments like "the eggs taste different" must never silently edit the version-tracked recipe record or otherwise act — this is the same consent-mechanic boundary already governing every other directive-producing surface in this doc. Any copy describing this feature needs the standing overpromising flag if it implies the product acted on a complaint rather than surfaced it.
- Physical tampering (a fake QR sticker pasted over the real one, redirecting to a lookalike page) is a real, separate attack class — mitigated mostly by process (recognizable branding on the landing page, tamper-evident stock) rather than by anything server-side; the one thing software controls is that the real page should never ask for anything sensitive.

### Two time-to-value channels — the QR/review path is meant to be fast, the causal-directive path is meant to be right

**Decided:** the founder wants the owner to feel real value the very first night the system is actually live, in his own words: *"i want them to feel it that night."* This resolves cleanly into two separate channels running at two different speeds, not a single unified timeline:

1. **Fast channel — light review-synthesis positive feedback, the $20 tier's feature.** As soon as customers start leaving reviews, the AI can synthesize an encouraging, aggregate "good job" signal for the owner that same night — this doesn't need to wait on the baseline/directive engine at all, since it isn't making a causal, per-variable claim, just reflecting real, current customer sentiment back to the owner in aggregate. This is also exactly where the customer-privacy promise above matters most: the owner gets the AI's positive synthesis, never the customer's raw words. Note this is NOT the full QR-code variable-binding system (that's $50-tier only, see "Billing model" above) — the $20 tier's version is lighter, correlating with reviews in general rather than tracking specific labeled environment variables.
2. **Slower, statistically-grounded channel — the causal directive engine, the $50 tier's feature.** This is the confidence-gated, baseline-dependent mechanic above (see "The confound problem, and its fix" and "Baseline timing" Proposal 4), paired with the full QR-code environment-variable system — it takes longer on purpose, because a causal claim ("the recipe change cost you 4 customers") needs real signal behind it. Speeding this one up dishonestly would undermine the whole confidence-gate design just built.

**One timing note worth being precise about, not silently glossed over:** "that night" most plausibly means the first night the restaurant is actually operating with stickers placed and scanned — not literally the night of clicking sign-up, since the stickers themselves take up to 24 hours to arrive by mail first (see the onboarding flow above). Worth confirming this is the intended reading rather than assuming.

### AI summary of the business — decided: renamed from "reviews," risk substantially reduced

The founder's original idea, verbatim: *"have to literally ai write reviews, express."* Every few weeks, the AI would post supportive, general commentary about the business, his own example: *"x restaurant owner has made the seating in his cafe very comfortable for customers."* This was flagged as high regulatory risk — content authored by a business's own AI and presented as an organic consumer review is squarely the kind of thing the FTC's rule against fake or AI-generated consumer reviews/testimonials (finalized 2024) targets; the exact current scope of that rule still needs independent verification before being treated as settled, but the risk category was real, the same class of risk that already got the labor/wage-surge idea shelved elsewhere in this doc.

**The founder's resolution: drop the word "review" entirely, call it an AI summary of this place instead.** This isn't just a rename — it changes the actual legal posture. A disclosed AI-generated summary describing a business (synthesized from real customer input, labeled as AI-generated) is not a fabricated customer testimonial, and doesn't carry the same fake-review exposure. Two things this rename does *not* automatically resolve, worth keeping in view rather than treating the whole risk as closed:
- **Where it posts still matters.** Posted on the business's own JustAddEgg page as clearly-labeled AI content, this is low-risk. Posted to a third-party platform like Google or Yelp — where users expect every entry to be an individual human's testimonial — the same fabricated-attribution problem returns regardless of what it's called internally. The founder's *"fuck google reviews"* framing doesn't specify a posting destination; if third-party platforms are ever in scope, this needs its own explicit decision.
- **Disclosure should stay real, not just a label change made once and forgotten** — the summary should read and be labeled as AI-generated commentary, not phrased to imitate an individual customer's voice ("I loved sitting here...") even under the new name.

## New engine mode, locked in concept, not built: Orchestrate/Test-Day mode

Arrived at during launch-day prep, building on an observation from a strategic review of the codebase: the confidence-gate engine (`generate-directive.js`) currently only reasons *passively* — it waits for a variable to change on its own (a note, a schedule edit) so it can attribute cause after the fact. This mode flips that.

**The mechanic:** the engine prescribes a single-variable, one-day experiment for the owner to run — "tomorrow, open the waffle station 30 minutes earlier and change nothing else," "feature the caramel topping today," "move the register line." A prescribed one-day test is the cleanest possible single-variable isolation the engine could ever get — better than waiting for a natural change, because nothing else is confounding it. It also lines up naturally with the engine's real changelog (see "What's real and deployed" — `variableDiffLog` is no longer permanently empty, `save-profile.js` writes a real diff on every tracked field change): a prescribed experiment that the owner then actually carries out by editing the relevant field IS a recorded change the moment they do it, feeding straight into the same real pipeline. This mode itself (the prescribing/prompting mechanic) is still concept, not built — only the changelog it would lean on is real today.

**Founder's own stated purpose for this mode, verbatim intent, two-fold:**
1. **Develop baselines faster** — a deliberate single-variable test converts baseline-building from "wait and hope something changes naturally" into an active, faster process.
2. **"Orchestrate to find causation faster and build context faster"** — over time this isn't just a one-off suggestion, it's a *sequenced* series of planned experiments the engine runs the business through, each one sharpening the business's own causal model faster than passive observation ever could.

**Guardrail, already standing, applies unchanged:** this mode may only ever prescribe **variables** — hours, menu emphasis, layout, pricing, what's featured — never a person's pace, timing, or conduct. Same line the no-employee-surveillance rule already draws elsewhere in this doc; this mode doesn't get a carve-out from it.

**Status: locked as a real, intended second mode. Not built.** Depends on the changelog/diff-log write path (see below) to actually record the prescribed test as a diff — build that first.

## New idea, proposed by founder, explicitly not yet locked: "Teach AI" mode

A third mode, raised in the same session, with the founder's own caveat attached and preserved verbatim in spirit: this must **not** stop the AI from making its own scientific discoveries, and must **not** override the AI's core goal of protecting/keeping the business alive. It is additive context, never a directive override.

**Sharper framing the founder landed on after the first pass (this is the defining goal, not just an example of use):** the mode has its own explicit goal — push the owner toward spending *less* time in the day-to-day. The actual teaching content isn't generic "how things should be done" — it's the owner saying, verbatim, in their own words, what the business would feel, look, and sound like if they walked away entirely. That articulation of the walked-away version of the business *is* the thing being taught.

**The insight underneath, founder's own words:** *"we are giving them the bones of franchising on purpose even if they won't [franchise]."* Franchising fundamentally requires converting tacit, founder-only operational knowledge into explicit, transferable, systematized knowledge — that's the entire hard problem of franchising. This mode produces exactly that artifact as a natural side effect of pursuing "give the owner their time back," regardless of whether the owner ever has any intention of replicating or franchising the business. The owner doesn't need to want a franchise for this to be worth building — the walked-away picture is valuable on its own, as the thing the AI measures the business against.

**The mechanic, as proposed:** the owner explicitly teaches the AI their own domain expertise and, specifically, this walked-away picture. This isn't training data for the causal-directive math (the confidence gate still only ever reasons from real logged data — "never fake precision" doesn't get a carve-out here either); it's underlying context that helps the AI understand **why** a given variable does or doesn't serve the core product, measured against the owner's own stated picture of the business running well without them.

**Founder's own stated purpose, still true and now a specific mechanism rather than a standalone goal:** protect the manager from — his own phrase — "eating themselves through passion project brain." A manager can pour real effort into something that feels important but doesn't actually serve the core product, with no outside check on that blind spot. The walked-away picture gives the AI a concrete reference to check variables against: does this serve the business the owner described running without them, or is it something only the owner's own presence and attention are propping up?

**Real, unresolved design tension — flagged here on purpose, not decided:** how does taught context avoid becoming a thumb on the scale for the causal engine? If an owner teaches the AI "the caramel topping is what people really come for," does that context help the AI ask sharper questions about caramel-related variables, or does it risk biasing a verdict toward confirming what the owner already believes rather than what the data actually shows? The safer shape, worth testing before building: taught context scopes *which questions get asked* and *which variables get flagged as worth checking against the core product* — never the causal verdict itself, which stays gated by logged data alone, exactly as it is today. Founder's call before this gets built.

**Status: concept only, explicitly not built, explicitly not scoped for today's build.**
